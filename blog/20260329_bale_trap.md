# The Trap Is Being Built Right Now

Iran has had no internet for four weeks. Not slow, not restricted. Gone. The only app that works is Bale, made by the Iranian government. Everyone knows what it is. Everyone knows what it's for. People are using it anyway, because they haven't spoken to their families in four weeks and there is nothing else.

And the remarkable thing isn't that people are using it. That's completely understandable, practically unavoidable. The remarkable thing is that the government doesn't need anyone to trust it. The trap doesn't require belief. It just requires that you need to reach your mother more than you need to hold a principle.

Getting out once you're in is very hard. Once your contacts are there, your family groups are there, your community is there, leaving means being alone, and that cost is borne entirely by you, while the benefit of a mass exodus requires everyone to move simultaneously, which no one can coordinate. The few historical cases where these traps have broken, like Eastern European regimes in 1989 or platform migrations like MySpace, tended to happen suddenly, after long invisible buildup, triggered by a shock rather than by people reasoning their way out. You can't count on that.

So the more useful question is: can the trap be prevented from fully closing? Yes, but the window is now, not later. The network effect hasn't compounded yet. Not everyone is there. Habits haven't fully formed. This is the moment where refusing costs the least, and it gets more expensive every week.

Some people have figured this out independently and are doing something smart: keeping Bale on a second phone that stays home, only used during blackouts, with no personal information on it. Using the web version instead of the app, which has far less access to your device. This is good. It maintains the separation between the surveilled channel and real life.

But the risk, and the people doing this seem to know it, is normalization through creep. You bring the second phone once because you might need it. Then you add one contact who's only on Bale. Then one group migrates there because it's easier. Then it's on your main phone because you're using it anyway. No single step feels like a decision. The cumulative effect is total.

The compartmentalization only holds if the boundary is treated as a rule, not a guideline. Rules don't require re-evaluation each time, which is exactly the point: the erosion happens in moments of convenience, when you're tired, when it's just easier. A rule doesn't negotiate. "The Bale phone stays home except during blackouts" is a rule. "I'll try to keep them separate" is not.

But individual rules aren't enough either, because the social graph doesn't wait for you. If enough people around you start using Bale for mundane things, your Bale contact list starts resembling your real social life, and the separation becomes socially costly to maintain even if you're personally disciplined.

The only thing that addresses the community-level drift is a community-level norm, something explicit and shared. Not a campaign, not a political statement, just an agreement: this channel is emergency infrastructure, not social infrastructure. "Why are you sending this here, use Signal." Mild friction. Naming the drift when it happens. Keeping the original reasoning alive rather than letting it fade.

The deeper problem is that the blackout itself is the leverage. When the internet is completely cut, this isn't a communication policy anymore. It's the weaponization of human social need. You can't ask people to endure silence with their families as a political act. That's not a realistic ask. The pressure is unbearable by design.

Which means the real target for prevention isn't user behavior during the blackout. It's shortening and reducing the blackouts themselves: through international pressure, through economic cost, through documentation of every instance, through making the tool expensive enough for the regime that they use it more sparingly. And it's building resilient infrastructure before the next one: mesh networking tools like Briar that work without internet entirely, satellite options for the people who need to stay connected most. None of this is fast. None of it helps tonight.

But here's what does matter right now: the trap works better when it isn't seen as a trap. The people who have invented the two-phone strategy, who are naming the normalization risk, who are maintaining Signal groups deliberately even when they're quieter, they're doing the right thing. Not because it solves everything, but because keeping the alternative alive and keeping the reasoning visible is exactly what prevents the bad equilibrium from becoming the only one. The cement isn't fully set yet.
