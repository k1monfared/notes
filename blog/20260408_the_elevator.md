# The Elevator

There are a few people in my life (mostly in the role of an advisor/mentor) that have given me a feeling that I haven't experienced anywhere else. These are people who are well accomplished and well recognized in the fields that we interact with each other, and yet somehow when I'm around them, they treat me in a way that I feel right next to them, as a colleague, peer, at similar level. Most of my mathematical advisors were like that, and now I'm noticing it in some other interactions I'm having with some other people. It is not being humble, they don't lower themselves, though it helps that they don't show off and sell themselves either, and when they need to do it they do it with charm and in a most pleasant way. But it is more of elevating me in a way that it feels genuine. I want to explore that and see if I can learn yet one more thing from them. 

---

## Two Load-Bearing Points

Before anything else, a few things need to be stated clearly because they run through everything that follows.

**1. Manipulation: This document is about skill, not character, and that distinction is a warning.**
The behaviors described here are learnable. Someone could read them and practice them deliberately, and in the short term the effect on others would probably feel the same as the real thing. But these behaviors only constitute elevation when they come from genuine interest in the other person, real curiosity about who they are, what they're becoming, where they belong, what they find meaningful. Without that, this document is a manual for a precise and useful form of manipulation: making people feel seen, capable, and included in order to serve your own ends. That possibility is not a footnote. It is the reason the precondition matters, and it is stated here first.

**2. Performing: Security as the precondition.**
You cannot genuinely elevate others while competing with them. The behaviors in this document are only authentic, and only work, when they come from a place of non-competition. Someone who is anxious about their own worth will inevitably use interactions to shore it up, which means the other person becomes an audience rather than a recipient. This presupposes a prior internal work of becoming secure enough that other people's rising doesn't feel like your diminishment. Where this precondition is missing, the practices become performances, and people feel the difference immediately.

**3. Flattering, the line here is honesty.**
Flattery inflates. Elevation reflects. The elevating person doesn't manufacture worth in someone, they notice and name worth that is genuinely there but that the other person may not be fully seeing. This distinction is not cosmetic. The whole edifice depends on actually seeing people clearly and caring about what you see. This is stated once here and returned to wherever the risk of slippage is highest.

---

## The Core Idea

Some people make you feel better about yourself simply by being around them. Not through flattery, not through performance, but through something structural in how they engage. This document attempts to map that something.

The central observation: for every relational dimension between people, there are **two ways to close a gap**. You can move yourself toward others, or you can move others toward you. Both close the distance. But they produce radically different experiences for the people involved.

Humility, the most culturally recognized version of this, is moving *yourself* downward to close a status gap. The complement, which has no clean name in English, is moving *others* upward. The humble person makes you feel warmly toward them. The elevating person makes you feel good about yourself.

This document explores four relational dimensions where this dynamic plays out, the nature of the two directions in each, their downsides, and concrete examples of what each looks like in practice.

---

## The Four Dimensions

### 1. Vertical, Status and Worth

**The gap:** People carry implicit senses of their own capability, intelligence, and worth relative to others.

**Moving yourself down (Humility):** You reduce your own apparent status, admit uncertainty, avoid performing expertise, make yourself approachable. Others feel comfortable around you. The social field becomes less threatening. The emotional result for others: *warmth toward you.*

**Moving others up (Elevation):** You treat others as more capable, insightful, or significant than they currently believe themselves to be, and you do this through your behavior, not through flattery. You ask questions that assume competence. You take their ideas seriously. You reference what they said as if it mattered. The emotional result for others: *they feel more capable and worthy.*

**The asymmetry:** Humility is a signal about *you*. Elevation is a gift to *them*.

**Downsides of humility:** Can slide into false modesty, self-erasure, or a subtle bid for admiration ("look how humble I am"). Can also leave the other person without a genuine peer, always meeting a lowered version of you.

**Downsides of elevation:** Can tip into flattery, which is felt as manipulative. Can project unrealistic expectations onto someone who then feels pressure rather than lift. Elevation without honesty is inflation, and people sense it. *(See load-bearing point 2 above, this is where the flattery risk is sharpest.)*

**The mechanism:** The most powerful elevating behavior is treating someone's contribution as if it *matters*, and letting that assumption become self-fulfilling. When someone consistently engages with your ideas as worth engaging with, you start to produce better ideas. The assumption creates the reality.

**Security note:** This axis is where competition intrudes most easily. Elevating someone's status feels natural when you're not tracking it against your own. If you notice yourself hesitating to credit someone, or softening genuine praise, that's often a signal of competition, conscious or not. The precondition applies here most directly.

**What this looks like in practice:** Someone asks you a question and instead of answering it, they first say "I'm curious how you'd think about this", and then actually listen. Or they reference something you said three weeks ago as if it had stayed with them. Or they disagree with you directly, as a peer, rather than softening it into a question, being argued with seriously is its own form of elevation. Or they give credit specifically: not "great work" but "that framing you introduced changed how I was thinking about the problem." The specificity is the proof that they actually saw you.

---

### 2. Horizontal, Belonging and Closeness

**The gap:** Social distance between people, the sense of being inside or outside each other's world.

**Moving yourself toward (Openness):** You make yourself vulnerable, accessible, and transparent. You close the gap from your side by allowing others in. The result: people feel warmly toward you, trust you, feel safe around you.

**Moving others toward (Inclusion):** You pull others into your world, you treat them as insiders, reference shared contexts that didn't previously exist, introduce them as belonging. You say "this is someone who gets it" before they've fully proven they get it. The result: people feel they *belong*, often in groups or spaces they weren't sure they were part of.

**The asymmetry:** Openness says "I'll let you see me." Inclusion says "You already belong here."

**Downsides of openness:** Can become oversharing or emotional burdening. Can feel like it obligates reciprocity. Radical self-disclosure without invitation can feel invasive rather than connecting.

**Downsides of inclusion:** Including someone before they're ready can create pressure rather than belonging. False inclusion, treating someone as an insider while the group doesn't actually accept them, can set them up for a harder fall. Inclusion has to be real or it backfires badly.

**The mechanism:** Inclusion works when it's *anticipatory*, you treat the belonging as already true, and the person rises to inhabit it. This is why great mentors, coaches, and community leaders make people feel they've arrived before they feel ready.

**What this looks like in practice:** You introduce someone not by their title but by something true about who they are, "this is someone who thinks seriously about X", before the group has had a chance to decide what to make of them. Or you use "we" in a way that quietly includes someone who wasn't sure they were inside the circle yet. Or you ask someone's opinion in front of others in a context where they didn't expect to be asked, treating their perspective as relevant before they've claimed it. The anticipatory move is everything, the person feels they belong *before* having to earn it.

### 3. Temporal, Identity and Becoming

**The gap:** The distance between who someone is now and who they could become; or between their present self and a richer, more coherent version of their identity.

**Moving yourself (Groundedness):** You are rooted, stable, historically coherent. Being around you gives others a sense of anchor. You carry your past lightly and authentically, which gives others permission to do the same.

**Moving others forward (Forward-orientation):** You address someone as who they are becoming rather than who they have been. You remember what they said they wanted to do, and you ask about it. You treat their aspirations as real rather than hypothetical. You speak to the future self as if it's already partly present.

**The asymmetry:** Groundedness offers stability in the present. Forward-orientation pulls someone into a better future.

**Downsides of groundedness:** Can anchor others too firmly to who they've been, making change feel like betrayal. Can become conservatism of identity, "you've always been like this."

**Downsides of forward-orientation:** Can project an identity onto someone that isn't really theirs. Can feel like pressure or unmet expectation if overdone. Works best when it's responsive to what *they* have already articulated wanting, not what you think they should want.

**The mechanism:** People often don't take their own aspirations seriously until (a form of [nonage](https://www.columbia.edu/acis/ets/CCREAD/etscc/kant.html) maybe?) someone else takes them seriously first. Being addressed as who you're becoming is a form of permission.

**What this looks like in practice:** Someone mentions in passing that they've been thinking about learning something, changing direction, or trying something new, and months later, you ask about it. Most people would have forgotten or treated it as small talk. The fact that you remembered signals that their aspiration was real enough to keep. Or someone has changed, grown, shifted, surprised themselves, and you meet the new version rather than narrating them through who they used to be. Or more subtly: you engage with someone's half-formed idea as if it's already worth taking seriously, which often gives them permission to take it seriously themselves.

---

### 4. Inside/Outside, Meaning and Membership

**The gap:** The distance between a person and something larger than themselves, a tradition, community, body of knowledge, or way of being in the world.

**Moving yourself (Humility before something larger):** You position yourself as a small part of a larger tradition or community. You don't claim to own the meaning, you point beyond yourself. This makes you seem trustworthy and non-threatening, and it models a certain kind of intellectual and spiritual maturity.

**Moving others inward (Induction):** You induct others into something larger. You say, explicitly or implicitly: *you belong to this too.* A great teacher doesn't just transmit knowledge, they make you feel like a participant in a tradition of inquiry. A great leader doesn't just inform, they make you feel like a co-author of something meaningful.

**The asymmetry:** Humility before something larger draws attention to the thing. Induction brings others into the thing.

**Downsides of positioning oneself as small:** Can evacuate your own authority when it's actually needed. Can be a deflection from responsibility. Can feel like you're refusing to lead.

**Downsides of induction:** Can feel like recruitment or manipulation if the "larger thing" serves your interests. Pseudo-induction, pulling someone into something that doesn't actually have room for them, is a betrayal. Works only when the membership is real.

**The mechanism:** Meaning is partly contagious. When someone treats you as already inside a meaningful thing, you start to inhabit it. The induction creates the membership.

**What this looks like in practice:** A conversation about a book or idea where the other person doesn't explain it *to* you but thinks about it *with* you, positioning you as a fellow traveler rather than a student. Or someone who says "what you're doing connects to something longer" and names the tradition or lineage, which suddenly gives your work more gravity than it had before. Or a leader who frames a team's effort not as executing a task but as participating in something that matters, and means it. The common thread is that the person making the induction genuinely believes the larger thing is real and genuinely believes you belong to it. Performed induction, where someone is going through the motions of meaning-making, is felt immediately and is worse than nothing.

## What the Dimensions Share

Each dimension has this structure:

1. There is a gap, of worth, closeness, time, or meaning
2. The gap can be closed from either end
3. Closing it from your end produces warmth toward you
4. Closing it from their end produces something better in them

The people who feel irreplaceable in your life are probably doing several of these simultaneously. Any one axis is something you can find occasionally. Convergence across multiple axes at once is rare, and that rarity is exactly why it's hard to name.

---

## A Note on Flattery vs. Elevation

This was stated at the top but bears repeating in the context of practice: the line between elevation and flattery is not about degree of warmth, it's about whether what you're reflecting back is real. Flattery invents. Elevation notices. The test is simple: could you defend the specific thing you said about someone if pressed? If yes, it's elevation. If you'd struggle to substantiate it, it's flattery.

The dangerous middle ground is *generic praise*, enthusiastic but unspecific. "You're so good at this" can feel good momentarily but doesn't land as elevation because it doesn't demonstrate that you actually paid attention. Specificity is the proof of perception.

## Conclusion: Precondition and Skill

This post has been about skill, the specific ways that genuine care for another person can be expressed so that it actually lands as elevation rather than just warmth. The four dimensions describe the terrain: where the gaps between people exist, how they can be closed from either end, and what it looks like when someone closes them in the direction that lifts others rather than themselves.

But the document has been quietly assuming something it hasn't fully named until now: that the person doing this actually cares. Genuine interest in the other person, real curiosity about who they are, what they're becoming, where they belong, what they find meaningful, is not one item on the list. It is the precondition for the list. Without it, you are not an elevating person who has learned some things. You are someone who has learned a set of techniques for making people feel seen, capable, and included, and is deploying them for your own ends. That is a precise and useful description of manipulation.

The reason this matters is that the behaviors described in this document are learnable. Someone could read the examples in each dimension and practice them deliberately. And in the short term, the effect on others would probably feel the same. The difference is not in the immediate experience but in what the relationship is actually for. Genuine elevation treats the other person's flourishing as the point. Performed elevation treats it as the instrument.

So what this post cannot teach, and does not try to, is how to become genuinely interested in people. That is a different and prior question. What it can offer is this: if the interest is already there, here is the shape it can take. Here is how it becomes visible to the person you're with. Here is where it lands and why.

The people you think of when you read this probably didn't learn it from a document. They became this way because they were genuinely absorbed in the people around them, and over time that absorption found its natural expressions. The skill followed from the character. That sequence matters, and it's the one this document cannot reverse.
